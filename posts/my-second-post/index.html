My Second Post

July 17, 2018

Very creative on these two post titles. Promise that’ll change for future ones (assuming they get written…).

This post’s my last one for a short while, I suspect. Call it my mini review of Margaret Atwood’s The Handmaid’s Tale.

I might be blasted by those numerically inclined (or parts thereof), but I’ll say that books can’t really be rated by a single number. So I won’t try to. Instead I’ll say that Atwood’s work of ‘speculative fiction’ is an excellent work that manages the impressive trick of feeling like the internal conversation, or narrative, of someone else’s mind in a way few can pull off. The way in which different memories bleed into each other, allied with authentically imperfect memory (unlike other works, cough Frankenstein cough), meant I related with the narrator remarkably well. Certainly I can envision living in life with such a voice in my own mind, different in style and content though it may be. This is actually a lot less easy than it sounds. In this novel you are not given a logical structure where things are cleanly separated, you follow the authentic rhythm of the narrator, the curiosities of their own mind and thought process. To maintain this style as consistently as Atwood does is testament to her talent as a writer.

The other main takeaway was probably realised about halfway through. The reason why, I think, Atwood’s society doesn’t sound as dystopian as one might expect given its hype and supposed genre is because it isn’t meant to be. Certainly there are elements, the Wall, the Colonies, the ceaseless threat hanging above those defying the state like the sword of Damacles. But nonetheless you are mostly hidden away from the details. I think this is intentional. It is a literary device alluding to how women were kept ignorant by a predominantly male elite, to keep them in a subservient state with respect to knowledge. This is reinforced by how women are denied from reading or writing in Gilead. Additionally, it’s scarier. It’s left to your imagination how violent things could get.

The thing that surprised me (don’t worry, I’m getting to my point), as was mentioned in the nicely-added post-Gilead(?) epilogue was how Gilead was quite “matriarchial in content”. Offred and the Handmaids commanded more power than perhaps you might have expected, or at least influence. They are dangerous to their Commanders and others should they break the law, as presumably was evident to the Commander after Offred was taken away. Women are also protected better in some ways than before, with the Commander reflecting on the improvement of ‘stats’ on how women are safer in Gilead than they were in earlier times.

The nutshell? I think The Handmaid’s Tale is a unique dystopian novel not in how horrifying its envisioned world is (1984 is far scarier, in my opinion), but more on how, at least in some sense, from women’s perspectives it is actually a reflection of how our own society is actually the real dystopia. The fact that this dystopian future even suggests that crimes as abhorrent as rape are less prevalent than in our own society is quite horrifying if you think about it. That makes it the more socially relevant between it and 1984, I think.

I still prefer 1984, because that novel left me feeling profoundly hollow after finishing it. It is borderline-iconic, definitive of the genre, many of its terms and themes have entered common language. But Atwood’s Tale deservedly stands alongside it as a great work in its own right, discovering a remarkably different and clever take on the genre.
